Case 01 · Business Context

The challenge was not only acquiring attention

Halio Sonic and CoBa's Daughter attract customers through creator content, affiliate links, paid media, organic social, events, product launches and marketplace discovery.

These users do not arrive with the same level of knowledge or intent. Some are discovering the brand for the first time. Some are comparing products. Some have added to cart but still have an unresolved concern. Others have purchased but have not yet formed a repeat habit.

A generic promotional approach cannot serve all of these users effectively.

Business challenge
Build communication journeys that preserve the brand promise while helping each user make the next relevant decision.
My role

Lifecycle strategy and execution across welcome, product education, cart recovery, purchase, post-purchase, replenishment and retention. I identify the customer state, shape the message and CTA, coordinate campaign timing, and connect email with product, creator, social and e-commerce activity.

Halio Sonic — promise

Consistent, credible at-home beauty technology between professional treatments.

CoBa's Daughter — promise

Sensorial, low-maintenance body-care rituals rooted in Vietnamese ingredients and heritage.

The content changes by stage. The brand promise should not.

Case 01 · Welcome & Consideration

Welcome is not a discount. It is the first decision journey.

User state: interested, limited knowledge, no purchase history Brand objective: positioning, trust, relevance Growth objective: first meaningful product interaction
Email 1
Establish the promise

Explain what the brand stands for and why it matters. Halio: at-home light therapy designed for consistency, not quick-fix promises. CoBa: Vietnamese-inspired body care that turns a daily shower into a low-maintenance ritual.

CTA → Discover the brand
Email 2
Help the user navigate

Reduce product confusion: shop by concern, shop by ritual, best sellers, product comparison, "which product is for me?"

CTA → Find your product
Email 3
Build trust

Add evidence and social proof: reviews, creator proof, ingredient or technology credibility, use-case demonstration.

CTA → See how it works
Email 4
Create purchase motivation

Convert interest into action: first-order incentive, launch benefit, bundle value, shipping threshold, seasonal urgency.

CTA → Start your ritual / Shop now
Main hypothesis
An education-led welcome journey can generate more qualified intent than leading immediately with a discount.
Experiment ideas
  • Brand-first vs discount-first Email 1
  • Product quiz vs best-seller navigation
  • Creator proof vs expert explanation
  • Four-email vs three-email flow
  • Benefit-led vs problem-led subject line
Case 01 · Abandonment & Conversion

High intent does not mean zero friction

Potential objections
PriceProduct fitCredibilityShippingUsage complexityNo urgencyNeeds social proof
Conversion principle
Do not use a discount to answer every objection.
Message 1
Return to the decision

Remind the user what they considered and restate the main value. Objective: bring the product back into attention.

Message 2
Resolve uncertainty

Answer the most common objection. Halio: how often should I use it, which device is right, does it fit an at-home routine? CoBa: what makes the refill better value, how does the scrub fit a weekly routine, what does it feel like?

Message 3
Add proof

Reviews, creator content, demonstrations, customer stories. Objective: reduce perceived risk.

Message 4
Introduce urgency or incentive

Limited offer, bundle saving, free shipping, early access, seasonal deadline. Objective: a reason to act now.

Main hypothesis
For premium products, benefit reinforcement and social proof should appear before aggressive discounting.
Tests
  • Proof before incentive vs incentive first
  • Review-led vs creator-led recovery
  • Single reminder vs complementary bundle
  • Static image vs routine demonstration
  • 24-hour vs 48-hour timing
Metrics
  • Recovery conversion rate
  • Revenue per recipient
  • Average order value
  • Unsubscribe rate
  • Time from cart to purchase

Case 02 · Persona & Job To Be Done

The user is not only booking a driver. They are protecting a customer promise.

Primary persona — small online seller, HCMC
  • Handles ~5–20 local orders per day
  • Sells through social media or marketplaces
  • Manages customer communication personally
  • Compares providers on speed, price, reliability
  • Limited time to troubleshoot delivery issues
Merchant success definitionThe order is accepted quickly, delivered safely and completed with minimal manual follow-up.
Functional job

"When I receive an order, I need to book a suitable delivery service quickly, so I can fulfil it without interrupting the rest of my business."

Emotional job

"I need to feel confident the delivery will not damage my relationship with my customer."

Social job

"I want my store to appear reliable and professional, even with a small team."

Platform opportunity
AhaMove can become more than a delivery option. It can become part of the merchant's daily operating habit.

Case 02 · Friction Analysis

Three types of friction shape retention

1 · Product friction

  • Too many service choices
  • Uncertainty about vehicle / service type
  • Repeated address & instruction input
  • No guidance for first-time users
  • Multiple decisions before first value

2 · Brand & trust friction

  • Will a driver accept quickly?
  • Is the displayed price final?
  • What happens if delivery fails?
  • Will the item be handled safely?
  • Who supports me when things go wrong?

3 · Growth friction

  • No clear reason to return immediately
  • Second booking may not feel easier
  • Business features stay undiscovered
  • Follow-up ignores actual booking status
  • Rewards create temporary use, not habit
Product friction slows the booking. Trust friction delays commitment. Growth friction prevents habit formation.
Opportunity
Retention begins before the first delivery is complete. The first experience must create confidence, predictability and saved effort.
Case 02 · Growth Hypotheses

Five hypotheses to move users forward

Hypothesis 1

Guided service recommendation

If first-time users answer 2–3 simple questions about item size, urgency and distance, a recommended service can reduce choice friction and improve booking completion.

Solution: "Help me choose" guided flowMetric: first-booking completionGuardrail: cancellation / service-change rate
Hypothesis 2

Reliability reassurance

If the confirmation screen clearly explains price, estimated matching time and support expectations, users feel more confident completing the booking.

Solution: fee breakdown + matching estimateMetric: confirmation rateGuardrail: support-contact rate
Hypothesis 3

Save merchant preferences

If merchants can save pickup locations, common notes and preferred settings after the first delivery, the second booking requires less effort.

Solution: "Save for next booking" promptMetric: time to 2nd bookingSecondary: 7-day repeat rate
Hypothesis 4

Book-again momentum

If the completion screen offers a relevant next action — book again, save customer details, create another delivery, explore merchant tools — users are more likely to form a repeat habit.

Metric: repeat booking within 7 days
Hypothesis 5

State-based lifecycle communication

If communication reflects whether the user registered, abandoned, completed or experienced a failed delivery, it will be more useful than generic promotional messaging.

Metric: journey reactivation / repeat booking

From Nutrition Anxiety to Guided Action

Case 03 · User Problem

The problem was not a lack of information

Vietnamese families have access to abundant health and nutrition content. But much of it is fragmented, technical, contradictory or hard to apply to everyday meals.

Parents may understand that nutrition matters — without knowing what action to take at the moment they choose, prepare or serve food.

Existing journey
Notices a health concern → searches Google / social → conflicting advice → tries a temporary fix → hard to maintain → returns to old behavior
Primary persona — working mother, 26–35
  • Needs: improve family nutrition · practical meal decisions · understand food without medical expertise · guidance that fits a busy routine
  • Frustrations: conflicting information · technical language · generic advice · time-consuming tracking · hard-to-sustain habits
Key insight
Information alone does not change behavior. Guidance must appear at the moment a user is making a food decision.
Case 03 · Product Proposition

Make healthy eating understandable and actionable

Proposition

Melius helps families understand food choices and take practical action — without requiring them to become nutrition experts.

Product hypothesis
If nutritional information becomes visual, contextual and personalized, parents are more likely to understand it and apply it in everyday meals.
Understand

Translate complex nutrition into clear visual information.

Decide

Recommend practical next steps based on household needs.

Plan

Turn individual food decisions into a manageable meal routine.

Improve

Help users observe progress and form healthier habits.

Brand principle
Supportive, not judgmentalClear, not medicalizedPractical, not overwhelmingFamily-focused
Proposed activation moment *

User completes their household profile, scans or selects a food item and saves one recommended action.
* Proposed activation definition for portfolio analysis.

Should-have

Mini Case 01 · Creator Activation

Creator activation as a partner journey

Scaling a creator program is not only a sourcing challenge. Each creator must move through a series of activation steps before they create value for the brand or platform.

Discovery → Qualification → Invitation → Acceptance → Sample request → Affiliate setup → Content creation → Posting → First conversion → Retention & rebooking
Main friction points
  • Invitation not opened
  • Sample request incomplete
  • Product received, no content
  • Affiliate invitation not accepted
  • Link or code not activated
  • Content posted without a strong CTA
  • Creator inactive after one campaign
Interventions
  • Clear onboarding communication
  • Stage-based reminders
  • Setup checklists & deadline nudges
  • Content and CTA guidance
  • Performance feedback
  • Rebooking & retention flows
  • SOPs and scalable tracking
Metrics
Invitation acceptanceSample-to-post CVRTime to first postActive affiliate rateFirst-sale rateRetained creator rate
Learning
Partner growth and customer growth share the same logic: reduce friction at each stage and make the next action clear.
Mini Case 02 · TYB

From community participation to first purchase

Community engagement can create activity without necessarily building product understanding or purchase intent.

Discover community → Join → First challenge → Earn coins → Learn about product → Visit shop → Add to cart → Purchase → Return & advocate
Initial challenge

General lifestyle prompts generated participation, but the path from engagement to purchase was not always explicit.

Strategy shift
Product educationIngredient discoveryRefill-value communicationSocial proofPurchase-oriented challengesShoppable CTAsIncentives tied to commercial action
Measurement
Join-to-first-challenge rateChallenge completionProduct-page CTRAdd-to-cart rateFirst-purchase rateRevenue / active memberRepeat participation
Hypotheses
  • Product-discovery challenges create stronger intent than general engagement prompts
  • Social proof from community members reduces uncertainty for first-time buyers
  • Rewards tied to meaningful shop actions create a clearer conversion path
Learning
Community should not sit beside commerce. Its activities should progressively increase knowledge, trust and purchase readiness.
Across every case, the same principle holds: find where the user hesitates, remove the friction, and make the next action obvious.
